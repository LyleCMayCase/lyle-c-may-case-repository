Where the Case Stands Today

Lyle Clinton May is a North Carolina death row prisoner convicted in 1999 of the murders of Valeri Sue Riddle and Kelley Mark Laird Jr. in Buncombe County, North Carolina. The North Carolina Supreme Court affirmed his convictions and death sentences in 2001.

More than twenty-five years later, the case remains the subject of active post-conviction litigation.

The current proceedings stem from a Supplemental Motion for Appropriate Relief filed in January 2026. The motion alleges that Mr. May's convictions were obtained in violation of constitutional protections and raises claims involving law enforcement misconduct, prosecutorial misconduct, withheld evidence, disputed forensic evidence, and the reliability of statements attributed to Mr. May following his arrest.

One of the central issues raised in the litigation concerns a confession attributed to Mr. May. According to the motion, the interrogation was not audio recorded or video recorded, and the resulting statement is being challenged on constitutional and evidentiary grounds. Mr. May has maintained his innocence since his arrest in 1997, and the disputed confession is the only known instance in which he is alleged to have admitted involvement in the crimes.

The Supplemental Motion further argues that Mr. May was young, severely intoxicated, suffering from mental illness, sleep deprived, and incapable of making a knowing and voluntary waiver of his constitutional rights at the time of questioning. Because the statement was not audio or video recorded, and because its reliability is actively disputed, it remains a central focus of the case.

On May 7, 2026, Buncombe County Superior Court Judge Marvin Pope reviewed the motion and found sufficient cause to order further proceedings. The court directed the State to respond to issues that include a court-ordered evidentiary hearing granted in 2009 that was never held, a 2012 motion for post-conviction discovery regarding the State Bureau of Investigation that was never heard, and allegations that exculpatory evidence was withheld from the defense before trial.

On July 14, 2026, Judge Pope granted the State's request for an additional 60 days to comply with that order. The extension moved the deadline for the State's Answer to August 24, 2026 and extended the deadline to calendar the matter for hearing to October 8, 2026.

At the center of the current litigation are questions concerning the integrity of the investigation, the handling of evidence, the reliability of witness testimony, and whether information favorable to the defense was withheld from jurors. The Supplemental Motion further argues that subsequent revelations involving the Asheville Police Department, Buncombe County Sheriff's Department, State Bureau of Investigation, and Buncombe County District Attorney's Office warrant renewed scrutiny of the evidence and procedures used in the case.

The case also presents an unusual procedural history. In 2009, a North Carolina court granted an evidentiary hearing on Mr. May's claim of ineffective assistance of counsel. According to current filings, that hearing has never been conducted. The Supplemental Motion argues that significant legal and factual issues raised during post-conviction proceedings have never received a full evidentiary review.

The litigation remains pending. The State's Answer to the Supplemental Motion for Appropriate Relief is currently due on August 24, 2026. Following the State's response, the matter is expected to proceed toward further court review and a hearing on the issues identified in the court's orders.

What Happens Next?

On July 14, 2026, the court granted the State an additional 60 days to respond to the Supplemental Motion for Appropriate Relief. The State's Answer is now due on August 24, 2026. After the Answer is filed, the parties may submit additional briefing and the matter is expected to proceed toward a hearing as directed by the court.
