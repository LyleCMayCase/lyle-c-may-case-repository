LYLE C. MAY CASE OVERVIEW

Last Updated • July 20, 2026

This briefing provides a concise overview of the Lyle C. May case for journalists, researchers, attorneys, professors, students, and other interested readers. It summarizes the current status of litigation, principal issues raised in post-conviction proceedings, and repository resources available for independent review.

Executive Summary

Lyle Clinton May is a North Carolina death row prisoner convicted in 1999 of the murders of Valerie Sue Riddle and Kelley Mark Laird Jr. in Buncombe County, North Carolina. The North Carolina Supreme Court affirmed his convictions and death sentences in 2001. More than twenty-five years later, however, the case remains the subject of active post-conviction litigation involving allegations of withheld evidence, disputed forensic evidence, investigative misconduct, prosecutorial misconduct, and unresolved court proceedings.

The current litigation stems from a Supplemental Motion for Appropriate Relief filed in January 2026. The motion raises claims involving the Asheville Police Department, Buncombe County Sheriff's Office, North Carolina State Bureau of Investigation, and Buncombe County District Attorney's Office. Among the issues raised are allegations that evidence favorable to the defense was withheld, that significant information affecting witness credibility was not disclosed to jurors, and that aspects of the investigation and prosecution violated constitutional protections.

One of the central issues concerns a confession attributed to Mr. May following an interrogation that was neither audio recorded nor video recorded. According to the motion, the written statement presented at trial was not derived from a recording of the interrogation itself. Mr. May has maintained his innocence since his arrest in 1997, and the disputed confession is the only known instance in which he is alleged to have admitted involvement in the crimes.

The Supplemental Motion also raises extensive claims concerning forensic evidence, blood evidence, and evidence handling practices relied upon during the investigation and prosecution. Among the issues raised are allegations that information favorable to the defense was withheld from trial counsel and jurors, including information affecting the evaluation of forensic evidence and witness credibility.

The motion further points to subsequent disclosures involving the North Carolina State Bureau of Investigation and forensic analyst Brenda Bissette, whose work was specifically discussed in the 2010 Independent Review of the SBI Forensic Laboratory conducted by former FBI Assistant Director Chris Swecker and District Attorney Ronald L. Wolfe. According to the motion, subsequent revelations regarding SBI laboratory practices, forensic reporting, and blood evidence analysis raise questions regarding forensic conclusions presented to jurors and the reliability of evidence relied upon during the prosecution.

The Supplemental Motion additionally raises questions regarding evidence handling practices and allegations involving law enforcement agencies and prosecutors involved in the investigation and prosecution. According to the filing, information developed after trial concerning undisclosed materials, evidence management, and agency conduct calls for further judicial review of the reliability and completeness of the evidence presented at trial.

The case also presents an unusual procedural history. In 2009, a North Carolina court granted an evidentiary hearing on Mr. May's claim of ineffective assistance of counsel. According to current filings, that hearing has never been conducted. The motion further argues that a 2012 request for post-conviction discovery involving the State Bureau of Investigation was never heard despite remaining pending for more than a decade.

On May 7, 2026, Buncombe County Superior Court Judge Marvin Pope ordered the State to respond to the Supplemental Motion and directed that the matter proceed toward further review. In doing so, the court specifically ordered the State to address issues including the unheld evidentiary hearing, unresolved discovery requests, and allegations that exculpatory evidence was withheld from the defense before trial.

Taken together, the allegations involve claims of withheld evidence, disputed forensic evidence, an unrecorded confession, unresolved discovery requests, and a court-ordered evidentiary hearing that, according to current filings, has remained unheld for more than seventeen years.

This repository was created to organize publicly available court records, legal filings, source materials, forensic reports, court opinions, and supporting authorities so that journalists, attorneys, researchers, professors, students, and members of the public can independently review the claims being raised and evaluate the underlying evidence for themselves.

Why This Case Is Receiving Attention Now

In May 2026, Buncombe County Superior Court Judge Marvin Pope ordered the State to respond to a Supplemental Motion for Appropriate Relief raising allegations involving withheld evidence, forensic evidence, witness credibility, evidence handling, and unresolved post-conviction proceedings.

The State's response is currently due on August 24, 2026. The court has also ordered the matter calendared for further proceedings, placing the case in active litigation for the first time in years.
