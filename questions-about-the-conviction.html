Questions About the Conviction

ISSUES RAISED IN POST-CONVICTION LITIGATION

Last Updated • July 2026

This page provides an overview of the principal issues raised in post-conviction litigation and advocacy surrounding the Lyle C. May case. The purpose of this page is not to determine guilt or innocence, but to summarize questions that continue to be the subject of legal review and public interest.

The Conviction Relied Heavily on an Unrecorded Confession

A central issue in post-conviction litigation is the confession attributed to Lyle May. According to court filings, the interrogation was not audio recorded and was not video recorded. The written statement presented at trial was produced after the interrogation and not from a recording of the interview itself.

The Supplemental Motion argues that Mr. May was young, severely intoxicated, suffering from mental illness, sleep deprived, and incapable of making a knowing and voluntary waiver of his constitutional rights at the time of questioning.

Mr. May has maintained his innocence since his arrest in 1997. The disputed confession is the only known instance in which he is alleged to have admitted involvement in the crimes.

Because the confession became a significant part of the State's case, questions regarding its reliability remain a major focus of post-conviction litigation and advocacy.

Questions Regarding Witness Testimony

The Supplemental Motion raises questions regarding the reliability of witness testimony used during the prosecution of the case. Post-conviction filings argue that certain statements presented to jurors should be reexamined in light of information developed after trial.

Post-conviction filings argue that information affecting witness credibility may not have been fully presented to jurors. Advocates contend that additional scrutiny of witness testimony is warranted in light of information developed after trial.

Witness testimony can play a significant role in criminal prosecutions, particularly when jurors are asked to evaluate credibility and conflicting accounts. The current litigation argues that certain witness-related information warrants renewed review and that jurors may not have received a complete picture of evidence affecting credibility.

Questions Regarding Forensic Evidence

The current litigation also raises questions regarding forensic evidence presented during the investigation and prosecution.

Post-conviction filings reference concerns involving forensic evidence and information that became available after trial regarding investigative and forensic practices within agencies involved in the case.

Advocates argue that these issues warrant renewed review of evidence relied upon to obtain the conviction.

Forensic evidence often carries substantial weight with jurors because it is frequently viewed as objective or scientific. The Supplemental Motion argues that certain forensic issues deserve renewed scrutiny in light of information that became available after trial.

Allegations of Withheld Evidence

The Supplemental Motion alleges that evidence favorable to the defense was not disclosed before trial.

These allegations include claims that information affecting witness credibility and other potentially exculpatory material was withheld from defense counsel and jurors.

Whether evidence was improperly withheld remains one of the issues currently before the court.

If proven, the withholding of material evidence can raise constitutional concerns because jurors are entitled to hear information that could affect the fairness and reliability of a verdict. The court is currently reviewing allegations that such information was not fully disclosed before trial.

A Court-Ordered Hearing That Never Occurred

In 2009, a North Carolina court granted an evidentiary hearing on Mr. May's claim of ineffective assistance of counsel.

According to current filings, that hearing has never been conducted.

The Supplemental Motion argues that significant legal and factual questions raised during post-conviction proceedings have remained unresolved for more than a decade.

The unresolved hearing is unusual because the claim survived judicial review and was deemed significant enough to warrant further examination, yet no evidentiary hearing has occurred.

Why Advocates Remain Concerned

Supporters, researchers, attorneys, journalists, and death penalty abolition advocates continue to follow the case because it combines multiple issues frequently associated with wrongful conviction claims.

Among the concerns raised are an unrecorded confession, allegations of withheld evidence, questions regarding witness testimony, disputed forensic issues, and unresolved post-conviction proceedings.

The purpose of this page is not to resolve those questions, but to identify the issues that continue to draw attention to the case and that remain the subject of ongoing litigation.

While reasonable people may disagree about the ultimate merits of the claims, the combination of disputed confession evidence, allegations of withheld evidence, questions regarding witness testimony, forensic concerns, and unresolved post-conviction proceedings has caused the case to remain the subject of ongoing legal review and public interest more than twenty-five years after conviction.
